📡 Goals & Impact

Powerful observations at hand

Monitoring stellar variability (Be, variables, cataclysmic), proper motions in open clusters, asteroid parallax, and stellar occultations. Calibrated data with photometric precision <0.02 mag and astrometry <0.3".

Education and Outreach

Training students and amateurs as citizen scientists. Use of professional pipelines, light curve analysis, and proper motions. Inspiring young scientists in Brazil, Chile, and Argentina.

Citizen science

Anyone with a Seestar S50 can contribute. Observations are sent to a central hub, reduced, and made available to the community. First target: Eta Carinae during its 2025 periastron.

🔭 The Seestar S50 telescope

Affordable technology

50 mm aperture, CMOS camera with IRCUT filter. Operates in Alt-Az or equatorial mode (wedge). Sensitivity similar to Gaia BP filter, enabling precise photometry down to magnitude 13.

What do we measure?

Light curves (stellar variability), precise positions (astrometry), and proper motions. Combining Seestar data with Gaia gives unique time series.

Data Hub

Online platform: https://andromeda.cbpf.br:5001 (under development). Upload your frames, receive calibrated data, and access light curves.

📊 Open clusters and proper motions

With the Seestar S50 we can measure proper motions of stars in open clusters, identifying members and studying the dynamics of the Milky Way. These data, combined with the Gaia catalog, extend the time baseline and allow detection of unresolved binaries.

Scientific and Educational Objectives

The T05-South project plans to use (initially) 30 Seestar 50 telescopes from ZWO for research, education, and outreach in Astronomy. Half of them will work as a network to conduct a variability survey of celestial objects observed in the disk and bulge regions of our Galaxy. The other 15 telescopes will be dedicated to performing observations, by amateur astronomers or the general public (guided by professional astronomers), in order to set up a pilot citizen-science project with pre-selected targets of scientific interest chosen by our team. Both initiatives will be coordinated by professional astronomers from different universities in Brazil, Argentina, and Chile.

The main goal of this initiative, including both the disk and bulge survey and the citizen-science project, is to collect data that will be useful for scientific advancement and publication of papers, while also serving educational and outreach purposes. The area to be covered in the disk and bulge survey by the first group of 15 telescopes will span 600 square degrees of the sky, selected from fields already observed by the Brazilian robotic telescope T80-South and the Southern Photometric Local Universe Survey (S-PLUS; see splus.cloud and splus.iag.usp.br). The fields will be centered on the equatorial coordinate system at RA=90° and DEC=−30° for the bulge, and RA=110° and DEC=−30° for the disk. For the citizen-science part, the first target will be the star eta car, in a pilot program aimed at bringing the community together around a significant astronomical event in 2025 – the periastron passage of the eta car binary system.

T05-South coverage map & citizen science network – interactive pipeline integration.

Given the qualities of the CMOS sensor used in the S50, it is possible to track the variability of pulsating objects, eclipsing binary systems, and objects that exhibit unpredictable brightness variations. Experience shows that with an aperture of only 50 mm and integration times of several minutes, it is possible to observe objects that are targets even for campaigns using the Hubble Space Telescope.

The eta Carinae 2025.7 periastron

Eta Car is one of the most luminous stars in the local Universe. It will go through periastron in late September 2025. Our goal is to get tens of data points during the astronomical twilights (evening and morning). The photometric peak is not explained by any existing models. This object will receive attention in the world press around the periastron, calling public attention to our monitoring campaign.

Simulated light curve of Eta Carinae (periastron passage 2025) – precision monitoring with Seestar S50.

Observing occultations caused by certain asteroids over background stars is feasible with these instruments. Some occultations produce effects that can last for tens of seconds, and observing these phenomena from different locations on Earth allows the determination of the dimensions and shape of objects in a way that is impractical even with direct imaging by large instruments.

Asteroid occultation profile: multi-site chord reconstruction using coordinated Seestar S50 telescopes.

Although challenging, the observation of exoplanetary transits that cause a decrease in the host star's brightness on the order of a few percent is feasible, as there are host stars that can be visible to the naked eye. Here, the challenging nature of such observations provides an opportunity to explore the limits of the capabilities of telescopes like the S50.

Exoplanet transit simulation: differential photometry with S50 – detectability of Jupiter-sized planets around bright stars.

Young stars are supposed to be close to their birthplaces and to have velocities still very similar to the initial ones. In this context stellar proper motions are fundamental to distinguish cluster members from field stars. By plotting a proper motion vector diagram one can easily distinguish the stars that share a common motion (cluster members) from those with random motions. We propose to observe open clusters to measure positions over time and compute proper motions, combined with Gaia for extended timebase.

⚙️ How does it work?

Seestar S50 – Smart telescope

The S50 integrates a 50mm f/5 apochromatic lens, Sony CMOS sensor, and built-in alt-az mount with field derotation. Using the intelligent image stacking (live stacking) and equatorial wedge, we achieve sub-arcsecond tracking and scientific-grade FITS files. Our team developed custom SDK routines to command exposures and dithering sequences for photometric calibration.

Calibration pipeline

Raw frames are automatically dark-subtracted, flat-fielded, and astrometrically solved with Gaia DR3. Aperture photometry yields differential light curves with precision down to 0.01 mag for stars brighter than mag 12.

Accurate astrometry & proper motions

By combining multi-epoch Seestar images spanning months or years, we measure stellar positions with centroid errors below 0.1 pixel (~0.3 arcsec). The time series is merged with Gaia epoch astrometry to derive proper motions and detect astrometric binaries.

Schematic: positional displacement over 6 months – proper motion detection in open clusters.

Open cluster membership

Using vector point diagrams (proper motion space), cluster members are separated from field stars. Our pipeline uses a Bayesian membership algorithm, delivering clean color-magnitude diagrams down to magnitude 15.

Example: proper motion vector diagram for open cluster IC 2391 – clear cluster sequence.

Real-time data graphics & public dashboard

All reduced data are ingested into the central hub (andromeda.cbpf.br) where users can visualize live light curves, FITS previews, and download calibrated catalogues. Interactive plots show variable star classification, asteroid motion residuals, and transit detection.
